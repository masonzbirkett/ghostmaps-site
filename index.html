The case for driving dark

This isn’t paranoia. It’s the public record.

Automated license plate readers were sold to towns as a tool against car theft. Here is what independent journalists, civil-liberties groups, and government audits have actually documented.

180 searches in 2 months

A Milwaukee officer used the department’s ALPR network to track a romantic partner and their ex nearly 180 times. It surfaced only because the victims looked themselves up — not through any internal audit.

SOURCE · Institute for Justice
4,000+ ICE-linked lookups

Local police ran thousands of Flock searches at the request of federal immigration authorities — giving ICE side-door access to a nationwide camera network it holds no formal contract for.

SOURCE · 404 Media
12M+ searches, mostly warrantless

An EFF analysis of more than 12 million Flock searches by 3,900+ agencies found police using the network for virtually any reason — including tracking protesters and people seeking reproductive care.

SOURCE · Electronic Frontier Foundation
4× a day, one resident

In one city, a resident found the local Flock system logged his car an average of four times daily — a quiet record of everywhere he went, available to any officer with a login.

SOURCE · ACLU of Iowa
Why this exists

The freedom to move without leaving a trail.

For most of history, walking out your front door meant something simple: you were gone, and no one knew where, unless they followed you. That quiet privacy — the right to move through the world unwatched — was never written down because it never had to be. It was just how life worked.

A camera on every corner doesn’t make you safer. It makes you legible — to whoever happens to hold the login, for whatever reason they decide is good enough.

GhostMaps doesn’t break any laws, hide any crimes, or interfere with any camera. It does one thing: it gives you back the option to take the road that isn’t watching. Where you go is your business. We built the map so it can stay that way.

The short version: we don’t collect anything

Privacy Policy

Last updated: June 2026 · Counter Surveillance LLC

GhostMaps collects no personal data. None. There is no account to create, no email to hand over, and no server that records where you go. Your location is used only on your device, only while you’re navigating, and is never transmitted to us or sold to anyone. An app built to protect you from tracking would be a contradiction if it tracked you — so it doesn’t.

What we collect

Nothing that identifies you. GhostMaps does not require or create an account, does not ask for your name, email, or phone number, and does not contain analytics SDKs, advertising trackers, or crash-reporting that transmits your data to us.

How your location is used

Your device’s location is used in real time, on your phone, to show your position on the map and calculate routes. It is processed locally and is not sent to any GhostMaps server, because GhostMaps does not operate servers that receive your location. When you close the app, that location data is gone.

We never sell or share your data

We do not sell, rent, trade, or hand your data to anyone — because we do not have it. There is no advertising network, data broker, or partner receiving information about you through GhostMaps. No account exists to be breached, and no trip history exists to be handed over.

Credits & the technology behind the map

GhostMaps stands on open, community-built work. Camera locations come from DeFlock and OpenStreetMap — crowdsourced, public datasets that GhostMaps simply downloads to your device. Those downloads are one-way and carry no information about you whatsoever. The 3D maps and turn-by-turn routing are powered by Mapbox; as with any map app, drawing a tile or calculating a route involves a request for that specific tile or route, and nothing in that request identifies you, ties to an account, or builds a history — because none exists. These providers act only as the rendering and routing engine, under their own privacy terms, never as a destination for your personal data.

Children’s privacy

GhostMaps is intended for general audiences and does not knowingly collect any information from anyone, including children under 13.

Changes to this policy

If this policy ever changes, the updated version will be posted here with a new date. Because the app collects nothing, we don’t anticipate material changes to what data is handled.
